Data wrangling

First I will limit the data set to the top 1000 names in a year and then calculate the relative risk.

library(tidyverse)

top1000 <- babynames %>%
  group_by(sex, year) %>%
  arrange(desc(n)) %>%
  mutate(rank = row_number()) %>%
  filter(rank <= 1000) %>%
  group_by(sex, name) %>%
  arrange(year) %>%
  mutate(relrisk = prop/lag(prop))

The only problem with doing it like this is that if there are gaps that the name made it in the top 1000, the calculation is off. For example, look at the name Aarush. Aarush was in the top 1000 in 2010, then dipped below the top 1000 in 2011 to 2014, and then back in the top 1000 in 2015.

Ok, letâ€™s try this again.

top1000 <- babynames %>%
  group_by(sex, year) %>%
  arrange(desc(n)) %>%
  mutate(rank = row_number()) %>%
  filter(rank <= 1000) %>%
  group_by(sex, name) %>%
  arrange(year) %>%
  mutate(relrisk = ifelse(year == lag(year)+1, prop/lag(prop), NA),
         loss_pct = (1-relrisk)*100) %>%
  ungroup()

The risk differnce

In public health we use both the relative risk1 and the risk difference. The usefulness of these measures is often the source of a lot of confusion in the media when there are stories about the benefit of certain treatments and screenings, for example, the benefits of mammography or the harms of alcohol use and birth control. Side note: one reason I love The Upshot is because they do an excellent job of explaining this to a non-epidemiologist audience.

The risk difference (RD)2 is the difference in proportionsâ€“it is the absolute effect of a risk factor, or the excess risk of disease among those who have been exposed to a vector vs.Â those who have not been exposed. In the study on alcohol use and breast cancer, a 40-year-old woman has an absolute risk of 1.45 of developing breast cancer in the next 10 years. If she is a light drinker, this risk becomes 1.51 percent. The risk ratio is 1.51/1.45 = 1.0413793, or a 4% increase in risk. However, the risk difference is 1.51-1.45 = 0.06, or an excess risk of .06 per 100. Thatâ€™s 1.2 people for every 2,000 light drinkers. When you put it in absolute terms, the RD brings a different perspective to certain types of risk.
