One analysis in the R community that has inspired me is Hilarly Parker’s analysis of the most poisoned baby name in US history. It turns out that I also have a so-called ‘poisoned’ name, or so I thought.

For a short period in grade school there was nothing I wished for more than for my name to not be what caused the adults to turn down the volumne on NPR. Its entrance into popular culture due to the Clinton Impeachment, a shoutout in Mambo No. 5, and also being name of the the least likable roommate on Friends, was unfortunate, I thought. But now I am fond of my name again. I’m also looking back on that cultural moment with a new lens thanks to Monica Lewinsky’s awesome talk.

After watching the talk, I decided that the only thing left to do was to dig into the data!

There are a lot of observations! Maybe we don’t need to calculate the relative risk for every name. We might want to remove some names that either have a small n or proportion. In Hilary’s analysis she looked at the top 1000 names in a given year, so I will follow that methodology.

One other difference is that we can now calculate the relative risk using the tidyverse.

Data wrangling

First I will limit the data set to the top 1000 names in a year and then calculate the relative risk.

library(tidyverse)
library(knitr)

top1000 <- babynames %>%
  group_by(sex, year) %>%
  arrange(desc(n)) %>%
  mutate(rank = row_number()) %>%
  filter(rank <= 1000) %>%
  group_by(sex, name) %>%
  arrange(year) %>%
  mutate(relrisk = prop/lag(prop))

The only problem with doing it like this is that if there are gaps that the name made it in the top 1000, the calculation is off. For example, look at the name Aarush. Aarush was in the top 1000 in 2010, then dipped below the top 1000 in 2011 to 2014, and then back in the top 1000 in 2015.

top1000 %>%
  filter(name == "Aarush") %>%
  kable()

2010 M Aarush 227 0.0001107 900 NA
2015 M Aarush 211 0.0001041 971 0.9404786

Ok, let’s try this again.

top1000 <- babynames %>%
  group_by(sex, year) %>%
  arrange(desc(n)) %>%
  mutate(rank = row_number()) %>%
  filter(rank <= 1000) %>%
  group_by(sex, name) %>%
  arrange(year) %>%
  mutate(relrisk = ifelse(year == lag(year)+1, prop/lag(prop), NA),
         loss_pct = (1-relrisk)*100) %>%
  ungroup()

It works!

In public health we use both the relative risk1 and the risk difference. The usefulness of these measures is often the source of a lot of confusion in media stories about the benefit of certain treatments and screenings, for example, the benefits of mammography or the harms of alcohol use and birth control.

The risk difference (RD) is the difference in proportions–it is the absolute effect of a risk factor, or the excess risk of disease among those who have been exposed to a vector vs. thsoe who have not been exposed. In the study on alcohol use and breast cancer, a 40-year-old woman has an absolute risk of 1.45 of developing breast cancer in the next 10 years. If she is a light drinker, this risk becomes 1.51 percent. The risk ratio is 1.51/1.45 = 1.0413793, a 4% increase in risk. However, the risk difference is 1.51-1.45 = 0.06, or an excess risk of .06 per 100. That’s 1.2 people for every 2,000 light drinkers.

Would the risk difference provide a new perspective to these poisoned names? Let’s calculate the risk differences in our babynames dataset.

rds <- top1000 %>%
  group_by(sex, name) %>%
  arrange(year) %>%
  mutate(riskdiff= ifelse(year == lag(year)+1, prop - lag(prop), NA),
         excessrisk_per10000 = round(riskdiff*10000,1)) %>%
  ungroup()

rds %>%
  filter(sex == "F") %>%
